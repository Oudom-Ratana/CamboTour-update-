Oudong Mountain (Phnom Oudong)

Kampong Speu Province

📍 40 km northwest of Phnom Penh, near Kampong Speu border
☀️ Year-round (best Nov–Mar)

Royal Capital of Cambodia (1618–1866)

Oudong Mountain (Phnom Oudong) is one of Cambodia's most sacred and historically significant sites. For nearly 250 years (1618–1866), it served as the royal capital of Cambodia, until King Norodom moved the court to Phnom Penh. The hill is dotted with stupas, shrines, and the remains of former palaces.

The mountain has three main peaks, each topped with stupas containing the ashes of former Khmer kings and important Buddhist relics. The most famous is the Damrei Sam Poan (Elephant Keeper) stupa, built in the 18th century. Another highlight is the Mak Prang stupa, which holds relics of Buddha brought from Sri Lanka. The hill also features a large reclining Buddha and panoramic views of the surrounding countryside and the distant Mekong River.

Climbing Oudong is a pilgrimage for many Cambodians. A long staircase with naga balustrades leads to the summit, passing monks' quarters and small shrines. Unlike the heavily restored temples of Angkor, Oudong feels raw and authentic – some structures are in ruins, overgrown with moss, adding to its mystical atmosphere. It's a place of deep spiritual meaning and a quiet escape from the city.

Visitor Guide

✓ Things You Can Do

  • • Climb to the three peaks (easy to moderate, 30–45 min)
  • • See royal stupas containing ashes of Khmer kings
  • • Visit the reclining Buddha and the Mak Prang relic stupa
  • • Enjoy sweeping views over rice fields and the Mekong
  • • Combine with nearby Kampong Speu palm sugar villages

✗ Things You Can't

  • • Climb or sit on the ancient stupas
  • • Wear revealing clothing (shoulders/knees covered)
  • • Litter or damage religious artifacts
  • • Enter restricted areas marked for monks

💡 Pro tip: Go early morning (7–9 AM) to avoid heat and crowds. Bring water and wear comfortable shoes. A tuk‑tuk from Phnom Penh costs about $25–30 round trip. Hire a local guide at the base for deeper historical insights.
